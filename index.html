01

Pompeii

In 79 AD, Mount Vesuvius erupted and buried the Roman city under metres of volcanic ash. Rediscovered in the 18th century, the site offers an extraordinarily preserved snapshot of daily life in antiquity — complete with plaster casts of the victims' final moments.

02

Ground Zero

On September 11, 2001, terrorist attacks destroyed the World Trade Center towers, killing nearly 3,000 people. The National September 11 Memorial now stands where the towers once rose — two vast reflecting pools set within the footprints of the original buildings.

03

Fukushima

On March 11, 2011, a massive earthquake and tsunami triggered a nuclear meltdown at the Fukushima Daiichi power plant. The exclusion zone remains partially closed, but guided tours now take visitors through abandoned towns frozen in time.

04

Auschwitz-Birkenau

Between 1940 and 1945, over 1.1 million people — mostly Jews — were murdered at Auschwitz-Birkenau. Today the former concentration camp stands as a UNESCO World Heritage site, drawing over two million visitors each year to bear witness to the horrors of the Holocaust.

05

War Tourism — Syria

Since 2011, the Syrian civil war has devastated entire cities. Organised tours now take visitors through the ruins of Homs and Aleppo — a controversial practice that blurs the line between bearing witness and voyeurism in an active conflict zone.

06

Human Safari — Sarajevo

Guided tours through war-scarred neighbourhoods of Sarajevo, where the 1990s siege left deep scars. Visitors walk past bullet-pocked facades and sniper alleys — tragedy turned into spectacle, raising questions about ethics and memory.

07

Chernobyl

The 1986 Chernobyl disaster was the worst nuclear accident in history. The city of Pripyat was evacuated overnight, leaving behind schools, hospitals, and an amusement park that never opened. Today, the exclusion zone draws hundreds of thousands of visitors each year.

08

Capuchin Catacombs

In the catacombs beneath the Capuchin monastery in Palermo, Sicily, over 8,000 mummified bodies line the walls — dressed in their finest clothes, arranged by age, gender, and social status. A macabre gallery of death that has drawn curious visitors since the 17th century.

09

Catacombes de Paris

Beneath the streets of Paris lie the remains of over six million people, transferred from overflowing cemeteries in the late 18th century. The ossuary stretches for kilometres through former limestone quarries — a subterranean city of the dead at the heart of the City of Light.
